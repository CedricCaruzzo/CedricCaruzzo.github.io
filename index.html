About Me

My research addresses the fundamental challenges of representation learning from large-scale, structured scientific data. I am particularly interested in developing novel machine learning methods that can navigate the immense scale and complexity of domains like digital pathology, where gigapixel images present unique optimization and architectural challenges.

I use this domain as a testbed to design and validate new self-supervised and multimodal learning frameworks. A key focus of my work is on domain generalization, developing models that learn representations invariant to systemic noise and batch effects, a critical step for robust real-world deployment. My goal is to create methods that are not only performant but also scalable and built on solid theoretical ground, fusing unstructured visual data with structured modalities like genomics or chemical representations to build more comprehensive models of complex systems.

Research Focus

Scalable Architectures for Gigapixel Image Analysis

Exploring novel inductive biases and efficient optimization strategies for vision transformers operating on whole-slide images, tackling issues of long-range dependencies and computational complexity.

Self-Supervised Learning for Domain-Invariant Representations

Designing contrastive and generative objectives to learn representations that are robust to nuisance variables, a key problem in learning from multi-source datasets.

Multimodal Learning with Structured and Unstructured Data

Developing frameworks to fuse high-dimensional visual data with structured information to learn powerful, predictive models of biological systems.
